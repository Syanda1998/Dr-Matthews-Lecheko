Overview

In this document I will address the five criteria that has been set out as the requirements for the Teaching awards portfolio. My Teaching Portfolio is based on my self-reflection and a critical analysis of my teaching and learning principles, beliefs, approaches, strategies, and current practice.


I primarily focuses on the outcomes of my experience as a lecturer in the Department of Environmental Health in the Faculty of Natural Sciences at the Mangosuthu University of Technology (MUT), which I joined in 2002 – 2006 and 2014 until now. However, my ideologies and approach to teaching and learning are the results of a development and refinement process over the last 11 years and academic development through studies focusing on being a better university teacher since I started to work in higher education. I have thought many subjects in Environmental Health


My knowledge development is categorised into two knowledge forms:

The first: Mode one of knowledge production which is the disciplinary knowledge that I have acquired as Environmental Health scholar and through the professional practice (guided by strict registration and affiliation with a professional board – HPCSA) as Environmental Health Practitioner. The second: Mode two of knowledge production which is multidisciplinary that I have acquired as a university teacher and as a researcher. My mode one has formed a good foundation for my mode two. My teaching and learning engagement is student centred.


My experience as a teacher in higher education comprises both teaching and some supervision in academic environment. My progression in my career as a University of Technology Teacher and Environmental Health Practitioner can be summarised as follows:

  • Lecturer ;2002 - 2004 in the Mangosuthu University of Technology (MUT) National Diploma in Environmental Health – teaching various modules in the department as allocated by the HOD.
  • Acting Head of Department (HOD) ; 2005 - 2006 in the Mangosuthu University of Technology (MUT) HOD duties and lecturing. The main reason for appointment as Acting HOD, I was the only lecturer in the department registered with the professional board for environmental health (HPCSA) as Environmental Practitioner with the master’s qualification. The professional board is very strict on registration in our profession. This was the most difficult period in out department; for the first time we had evening classes and a total number of 120 new students. Our Professional Board (HPCSA) was prompted to conduct a special audit of our offering. We managed to survive that process. All our students were accepted to be registered as Environmental Health Practitioners (EHPs). The HOD post was advertised. I was the only candidate who was interviewed, my current HOD was part of the panel coming from DUT representing the profession. The outcome was that I should continue to act and be supported by the Dean of the faculty until such time a decision is taken.
  • Researcher in University of Cape Town (UCT); 2007. I was appointed as a researcher in UCT, while registered as a PhD student. Short stay due to financial and family commitments.
  • Environmental Health Practitioner; 2008 – 2013. I worked for the department of Health and the Alfred Nzo District Municipality.
  • Lecturer 2014 until now; teaching four modules and participating in all other duties of academic staff members.

My teaching and learning experience has grown over the years. I also visit other institutions as a visiting lecturer to hone my teaching skills. I also invite engage visiting lecturers to arouse curiosity in my students. Over the years we have formed a group with colleagues from other departments to work together in other to improve our teaching and learning skills.

Philosophy of teaching

The Forth Industrial Revolution (4IR), the information age, when the entire body of knowledge is available regardless of the classroom and textbooks, the role of education has rapidly changed; what in ages past could be obtained only in the classroom or textbooks is now in reach within no time. However, the role of a teacher as a facilitator of learning has become increasingly important and crucial. In the sciences, this importance has rarely been more pronounced: the world is replete with ideas and information, but it is impossible to sort those without the broad comprehension achieved through truly deep learning. Therefore, in a world and community confronted with the complex issues of pandemics like Covid 19, climate change recent floods in KZN, global food crises, genetic engineering, and environmental degradation, science education has never been more important. Certainly, I believe Environmental Health is a necessary education for the 21 century.


My philosophy of teaching is based on comprehensions derived both from my own experience and from the current scholarship of teaching and learning.


Over the years I have learned to engage my students in deep learning. Deep learning as an active, intellectually rigorous process that requires meaningful engagement with the learning materials.


Deep or significant learning begins with curiosity, so the first and most important step in encouraging meaningful learning is instilling in my students a sense of sincere, personal curiosity. I involve my students in relevant projects that arouse their curiosity. To achieve this, I begin every lecture with real-world, relevant topics that are meaningful to Environmental Health students, such as exploring the current environmental health issues.


For example, this year I started by focusing on Covid 19 as environmental health problem – spreading mechanism and controls and KZN floods, destruction of housing more especially informal settlements and housing them in shelters. The vital characteristic of deep learning is the personal ownership of learning based on students’ personal experiences, and so most of the class time is spent on students actively investigating and finding the answers to complex environmental health issues.


Through this technique, students truly own the lesson and apply that same inquiry process to more varied and broader problems, which is one of the most powerful aspects of active learning and the flipped classroom. In this teaching framework, my role is to open the door and let my students see the environmental health issues that need critical thinking.


Teaching and Learning is a demanding intellectual act requiring extensive preparation and scholarship distinct from control of the subject material.


Most of my teaching methods follow inquiry-based ideas: rather than giving students answers to complex issues, we work through issues as collaborators, and I step in primarily to expand on critical topics and address major misconceptions. A key element of effective teaching is thoughtful assessment design, especially the inclusion of a variety of frequent and informal assessment techniques. This means that much of my assessment consists of asking students to not only present an idea to me, but to explain where that idea comes from, the evidence that supports it, and how it might be applied to new situations. Classroom discussions play an essential role in my assessment of students’ learning, as they allow the entire class to work together towards the solutions to the initial problems or questions of a day’s classroom time. Another aspect of my teaching that has intensely increased student learning is the explicit consideration of not only my own course but also its role in the entire Environmental health curriculum, an entirely holistic approach that focuses primarily on developing skills that are expected in higher-level classes. These key strategies make up the framework of my approach to fostering excellent undergraduate educational experiences.


Curriculum design within the framework of significant learning, which emphasizes rich learning experiences, multi-faceted learning beyond simple content delivery, and skills-based learning goals.


The combined holistic teaching and curriculum design approach, have the potential to foster deep and powerful learning that prepares students not only for their degrees and careers, but also for a life beyond the classroom as flexible, self-directed learners. In a world characterized by rapid change and complex issues, I firmly believe this is one of the most powerful things that an admirable environmental health programme can present.


I have worked with many diverse student groups and professionals in terms of number of learners, age, cultural background, expectations, content and aim of the learning activity. This has required a continuous complexity and customisation of my teaching approach and methods. In fact, as observed by Ramsden (1992:102), “good teaching is open to change”. This is a key principle in the transition towards the “Learning Paradigm” (Barr and Tagg, 1995: 13), which is related to the evolution of the function of universities from institutions essentially for teaching to institutions responsible for learning, joining teaching and research (CHE, 2004).


My approach is oriented to let students understand environmental health as integrated complex systems, debating each topic through a holistic approach that addresses problems from a variety of perspectives, and relates environmental health aspects to environmental, social and economic implications, which are the three pillars of the sustainable development (UN, 2000).


Since I joined MUT, I have been focusing my practice on the South African and International realities and relevant challenges that need to be faced in the Environmental Health Services and in the Department of Health. I have also put effort into linking the criteria of social sustainability with the South African ubuntu philosophy, according to the “Africanization” goal for South African higher education promoted by Higgs and van Wyk (2006).

Teaching Approaches

Since I joined MUT as a lecturer, I have dedicated great effort into improving teaching and learning practices within the Environmental Health curriculum. The transition towards a transformative learning-based model in South African higher education has been supported by the development of institutional Teaching and Learning strategies able to lead the change (CHE, 2004). The improvement of my teaching methods has been influenced by the completion of the Post Graduate Diploma in Higher Education, particularly the modules on Teaching and Learning, Assessment and Research Supervision.


I have adopted a completely open-door policy so that if my students need further assistance, I am always available to provide further clarification, explanation, especially to motivate them, encourage their thinking, reflection and discussion. During the contact sessions I clarify and highlight the purposes and intended outcomes of each session and learning task.


I always try to inspire their critical thinking, making connections with previous knowledge and other modules, thus trying to maximise their awareness of their gradual knowledge and skill development. I often highlight those problems that must be assessed by multiple perspectives and in the municipalities and the department of health. Students must critically think about the problem and find their own way to face it, with a combination of understanding, gradual knowledge creation, critical thinking, passion and creativity.


I regularly alternate during the sessions the teaching mode, switching from formal lecturing and audio style to more visually-driven methods e.g., video presentation, AnswerGarden, slideshow and virtual – from zoom to Teams.


Curriculum Development

I have arrived in MUT for the second time in 2014, finding the Department of Environmental Health in the transition of their offering from the National Diploma in Environmental Health (3 years) to Bachelor of Science in Environmental Health (4 years). I have been working with the colleagues in the department to develop the current curriculum. We have addressed this matter as a teamwork. The continuous debate with the colleagues regarding the current curriculum review for accreditation purposes, new curriculum development, module development and review, gap analysis, identification of strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats for our programme and in relation to its future development has proceeded over the last four years.


I believe that our curriculum is based and reshaped by sustainable development goals and adapted to fulfil the requirements of the contemporary and future Environmental Health challenges and to develop higher-level skills for our graduates to work more effectively in the industry.


I have attended and proactively contributed to all the workshops and staff meetings that we have organised for team working on curriculum development and accreditation purposes. I personally developed materials for four modules for the BSC programme (Sustainable development 101, Environmental Health Promotion 02, Environmental Health Law, and legal Process III and Environmental Health Law, and legal Process IV).


My Head of Department (HOD) and other academic staff members are aware of my significant and robust contribution to the curriculum development process and teamwork. PGDIHE modules included the “Designing and Evaluating Curricula in Higher Education” this module helped me to understand curriculum in higher education better.
