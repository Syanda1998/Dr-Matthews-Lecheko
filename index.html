Philosophy of teaching

The Forth Industrial Revolution (4IR), the information age, when the entire body of knowledge is available regardless of the classroom and textbooks, the role of education has rapidly changed; what in ages past could be obtained only in the classroom or textbooks is now in reach within no time. However, the role of a teacher as a facilitator of learning has become increasingly important and crucial. In the sciences, this importance has rarely been more pronounced: the world is replete with ideas and information, but it is impossible to sort those without the broad comprehension achieved through truly deep learning. Therefore, in a world and community confronted with the complex issues of pandemics like Covid 19, climate change recent floods in KZN, global food crises, genetic engineering, and environmental degradation, science education has never been more important. Certainly, I believe Environmental Health is a necessary education for the 21 century.


My philosophy of teaching is based on comprehensions derived both from my own experience and from the current scholarship of teaching and learning.


Over the years I have learned to engage my students in deep learning. Deep learning as an active, intellectually rigorous process that requires meaningful engagement with the learning materials.


Deep or significant learning begins with curiosity, so the first and most important step in encouraging meaningful learning is instilling in my students a sense of sincere, personal curiosity. I involve my students in relevant projects that arouse their curiosity. To achieve this, I begin every lecture with real-world, relevant topics that are meaningful to Environmental Health students, such as exploring the current environmental health issues.


For example, this year I started by focusing on Covid 19 as environmental health problem – spreading mechanism and controls and KZN floods, destruction of housing more especially informal settlements and housing them in shelters. The vital characteristic of deep learning is the personal ownership of learning based on students’ personal experiences, and so most of the class time is spent on students actively investigating and finding the answers to complex environmental health issues.


Through this technique, students truly own the lesson and apply that same inquiry process to more varied and broader problems, which is one of the most powerful aspects of active learning and the flipped classroom. In this teaching framework, my role is to open the door and let my students see the environmental health issues that need critical thinking.


Teaching and Learning is a demanding intellectual act requiring extensive preparation and scholarship distinct from control of the subject material.


Most of my teaching methods follow inquiry-based ideas: rather than giving students answers to complex issues, we work through issues as collaborators, and I step in primarily to expand on critical topics and address major misconceptions. A key element of effective teaching is thoughtful assessment design, especially the inclusion of a variety of frequent and informal assessment techniques. This means that much of my assessment consists of asking students to not only present an idea to me, but to explain where that idea comes from, the evidence that supports it, and how it might be applied to new situations. Classroom discussions play an essential role in my assessment of students’ learning, as they allow the entire class to work together towards the solutions to the initial problems or questions of a day’s classroom time. Another aspect of my teaching that has intensely increased student learning is the explicit consideration of not only my own course but also its role in the entire Environmental health curriculum, an entirely holistic approach that focuses primarily on developing skills that are expected in higher-level classes. These key strategies make up the framework of my approach to fostering excellent undergraduate educational experiences.


Curriculum design within the framework of significant learning, which emphasizes rich learning experiences, multi-faceted learning beyond simple content delivery, and skills-based learning goals.


The combined holistic teaching and curriculum design approach, have the potential to foster deep and powerful learning that prepares students not only for their degrees and careers, but also for a life beyond the classroom as flexible, self-directed learners. In a world characterized by rapid change and complex issues, I firmly believe this is one of the most powerful things that an admirable environmental health programme can present.


I have worked with many diverse student groups and professionals in terms of number of learners, age, cultural background, expectations, content and aim of the learning activity. This has required a continuous complexity and customisation of my teaching approach and methods. In fact, as observed by Ramsden (1992:102), “good teaching is open to change”. This is a key principle in the transition towards the “Learning Paradigm” (Barr and Tagg, 1995: 13), which is related to the evolution of the function of universities from institutions essentially for teaching to institutions responsible for learning, joining teaching and research (CHE, 2004).


My approach is oriented to let students understand environmental health as integrated complex systems, debating each topic through a holistic approach that addresses problems from a variety of perspectives, and relates environmental health aspects to environmental, social and economic implications, which are the three pillars of the sustainable development (UN, 2000).


Since I joined MUT, I have been focusing my practice on the South African and International realities and relevant challenges that need to be faced in the Environmental Health Services and in the Department of Health. I have also put effort into linking the criteria of social sustainability with the South African ubuntu philosophy, according to the “Africanization” goal for South African higher education promoted by Higgs and van Wyk (2006).

Approaches to assessment and feedback

To foster higher order thinking and encourage independent learning, I give a wide array of assessments namely, Multiple choice, Projects, Group work, Tests, Assignments, and Presentations. These assessments are administered differently but blooms taxonomy is used to grade the level of questions. The type and purpose of my assessments vary due to the circumstance and needs of my students. I give both formative and summative assessments – Some assessments are meant to foster certain skills such as teamwork, some are meant to encourage student to do introspection etc. In this section, I go through some examples of the assessments I give my students and how and when, and for what purpose I administer them. I further explain the form of feeback given after each type of assessment.


Blooms taxonomy was used to grade the level of questions to ensure that the formative and summative assessments were pitched at the correct level. An attempt was made to ensure that at least 60% of the questions were based on concepts taught in class. The remainder of the questions varied containing problem-solving skills that students needed to analyse and apply based on concepts taught in class. At least 10% of the marks came from questions that required students to create new knowledge based on their existing knowledge


Formative assessments to foster higher level thinking: To foster cross pollination of ideas and knowledge sharing amongst student peers, I usually give my students formative assessments in the form of group projects. Each group projects is then evaluated and critiqued by other group members while providing feedback. This change of role fosters the engagement of higher order thinking skills since it requires students to look at the subject from a totally different angle as they evaluate each other’s submissions. While taking cognisance of the feedbacks, I then provide model answers aimed at helping the students to relook at their initial solution approach or design. This approach allows students to have two types of feedback – firstly the feedback on their submission and secondly the feedback on their evaluation. These types of feedback invoke student learning in ways that are unique and beneficial to the students.


Assessments to develop teamwork skills: Environmental Health involves working in teams of different stakeholders. Therefore, to promote the development of team skills, I adopt a group project teaching and learning approach. Students are expected to form their own groups using a set of stipulated rules: each group is made up of 5 or 6 members, contain two females or more and three males (due to the demographics of the class). This method was used to ensure that all the groups were at a similar academic level and as a way of making sure that students at risk learn from their peers. The group dynamics allowed students to cross-pollinate ideas and find solutions amidst conflicting ideas. The fourth years that do their research work in groups of two. Through an intensive evaluation of the group projects approach to teaching, I found that giving students a one-month deadline to complete the case study was futile since most students completed their work in the last week of the due date. As a result, I changed how the tasks were assessed giving students clearly defined milestones to achieve at the end of a week. If a case study was given four weeks to complete, each week had a deliverable for submission, hence causing them to be actively involved in the project over the three or four-week process. Furthermore, to inspire academic and professional discipline, I designed a peer evaluation form that allows student to rate group members after a group project. The result of this evaluation is then used to determine the contribution of each group member towards the success of the project. This encourages all students to participate in group assignments and think independently rather than rely on the group members. Feedback for these types of activities is given in the form of discussions in class.


Self-Evaluation Assessments: Mock examination: Before examinations, I ensure that adequate preparation is made by my students by giving them a self-evaluation mock exam, where students are expected to write a 3-hour paper under exam conditions. At the end of the mock exam, students are provided with the model answers and an elaborate explanation of what was expected. This mock exam is aimed at giving students an opportunity for self-introspection by assessing their own readiness before the actual exam.


Summative Assessments: To evaluate student learning at the end of an instructional unit, summative assessment are conducted. To meet the required standard with regards to the level and phrasing of question, as well as ensuring that the duration is adequate for students to complete the assessment, all assessments are internally moderated. At the end of each assessment students are given a marking grid to help them understand how the marks were distributed in a more detailed manner compared to the mark allocation that appeared after each question on the question paper. Furthermore, after marking the script, the marking guide is used as feedback to the student onto which a clear explanation is given to indicate which aspect of their computer program was incorrect. Summative assessments are usually in form of tests, which can either be practical or theory.


Assessment strategies and tasks “can offer rich teaching and learning opportunities for both teachers and learners” (Spiller, 2012: 3). I believe that if effective assessment strategies are implemented, these can provide guidance and feedback to learners and help them enhance their ability, identify their strengths and weaknesses, and develop confidence in a subject and in their learning process. Teachers can also obtain valuable feedback for improving their quality of teaching and the relevant learning produced: as observed by Ramsden (2003), assessment is an integral part of teaching and is critical for improving teaching.


Support for student development

In the beginning of the term I always inform my students about consultation times and I have posted a schedule on my door. I use consultation time so that I can provide extra support to groups or individual students. The one on one support is always effective since it addresses the specific needs of the students concerned. My philosophy on assisting students at risk is proactive rather than the usual reactive approach of analyzing student performance after an assessment. By definition, risk assessment ought to be done before undertaking a task. Therefore, in addition to the official protocol of identifying students at risk , I also use proactive tools such as platform quizizz.com and the discursive forum answergarden.ch. With these two platforms, I’m able to adjust my teaching content and methods before its too late. Quizizz.com gives me quantitative information on students at risk before I engage with them whilst answergarden.ch allows student to express themselves more hence i get qualitative information on what sort of interventions are required. To assist student at risk, I do one-on-one or group consultations. I also recommend additional material such as videos. I noted that having to wait until the official lists of students at risk will be too late for any meaningful intervention. In this section, however, I will not dwell much on this usual rhetoric, instead I will present evidence and student testimonies based on my belief that that my responsibility as a teacher does not end with the basics as stipulated in the job description. Influenced by this personal philosophy, I present evidence that demonstrate how as a lecturer I go an extra mile by (i) supporting students after graduation (ii) Develop students interested in entrepreneurship (iii) exposing students to industry & community projects. (iv) being a life coach and role mode


I also believe that it is important to develop students holistically for them to make the correct decisions in life. Apart from the academic projects that are given to the students to enrich them in Environmental Health, I find time in class, to speak to students about financial matters and the need to save after they have been employed. During these discussions I get to understand the students better, learning about their backgrounds and the challenges they are faced with in everyday life


Scholarly activities that influence and enhance teaching and learning

The field of Environmental Health has evolved over the years (from producing Environmental Health Officers to Practitioners). Furthermore, transitioning from offering diplomas to Bachelor of Science in Environmental Health.; hence it is imperative to be up to date with not only the current trends in the field but the current trends in teaching the subject. It is thus essential as an academic, to engage in scholarly activities. In this section, I present scholarly activities that have shaped the way I teach and develop learning material.


I took up the exciting challenge by informally sharing my personal experiences and learnt from experiences of my experienced colleagues, teaming up with colleagues in Analytical Chemistry, Maths and Biomedical sciences. This collaboration aimed at sharing teaching experiences and coaching each other in areas that need strengthening.


Furthermore, as a lecturer, I was keen on investigating the factors that affect academic performance of my students. The results revealed that students who are pre-occupied with other activities on campus struggle to make it through first year. The findings from my investigation has informed my approach when talking to students. Now I use a variety of methods to motivate my students. I use examples that help them to see and understand the social issues in their environment. In Environmental Health Promotion I engage them in projects that are based on health education. In the topics that I give them, I target to assist them.


I always participate in monitoring our students during work integrated learning (WIL). During my visits to different stakeholders that take our students, I always interview them in order to improve my teaching practice.


Some of subjects that I have thought needed laboratory experiments, I always had to work them out first and see if they will be workable. It demanded a lot of work in the laboratory for preparations.
